EdTech products built without local context tend to land badly in markets far from where they were designed. The assumptions embedded in a curriculum — about prior knowledge, cultural reference points, classroom dynamics, and what makes an activity engaging — are often invisible to the team that built it and immediately visible to the educators and students it was designed for. The Ethafa project was built on the right instinct: a Swiss STEAM education startup that wanted to engage Arabic-speaking girls in technology disciplines, and knew they needed regional expertise to get the educational design right.

## The Problem

Ethafa — a startup incubated through FabLab SUPSI at the University of Applied Sciences and Arts of Southern Switzerland — developed a STEAM education kit based on electronics, playful activities, and storytelling. The concept was strong: make technology education hands-on, narrative-driven, and culturally resonant. But the product had been developed in a Swiss context, and the team knew that the Arabic-speaking girls they wanted to reach would need more than a translated instruction manual.

They needed local context, pedagogical refinement, and evidence from real users — educators who worked with the target age group in Bahrain's schools. They also needed a partner with credibility in the regional education ecosystem who could provide that user testing infrastructure.

## What I Built

I led Fab Lab Bahrain's contribution to the collaboration, working with the Ethafa team to refine their educational materials and pedagogical approach through structured workshops with primary school educators in Bahrain.

The workshops brought together local teachers with the Ethafa kit, creating a structured testing environment where I could observe how educators interpreted the materials, where the activities stalled, and where the cultural or pedagogical assumptions didn't translate. That direct user feedback — not a survey, but observed, facilitated sessions with practitioners — fed back into the product's content and interaction design.

My team also contributed to adding local context to the educational resources: references, examples, and narrative elements that made the storytelling and electronics activities more immediately relevant to Arabic-speaking students. The curriculum refinements addressed both content (what was being taught) and didactics (how it was structured and sequenced to maximise engagement and learning transfer).

The output was a refined educational kit with validated content and a regional usability baseline — evidence that the product worked in the context it was designed to reach, not just the context in which it was developed.

## The Outcome or Impact

Ethafa was awarded a scholarship and grant by the GRS Foundation to manufacture their kit — a direct result of the validated product and the credibility of the user testing evidence. They subsequently launched a Kickstarter campaign, reaching a broader manufacturing and distribution milestone. The total funding secured was €150,000.

Eight participants engaged across the workshop and testing program. The collaboration demonstrated what a regionally embedded Fab Lab can contribute to an international product development effort: not just fabrication infrastructure, but the pedagogical expertise, user access, and cultural knowledge needed to make an education product work where it matters.
