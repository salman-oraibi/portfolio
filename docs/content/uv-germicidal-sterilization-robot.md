When COVID-19 hit, the window between "we need this" and "this needs to exist" compressed to weeks. UV-C sterilization was well-understood in controlled hospital environments but had never been packaged into an autonomous mobile system accessible to the range of facilities suddenly requiring contactless disinfection. I led the Archetype Foundry team that designed, engineered, and fabricated a working UV Germicidal Sterilization Robot — a self-navigating mobile platform combining robotics, vision processing, and UV-C technology into a deployable product.

## The Problem

Manual surface disinfection is time-consuming, inconsistent, and exposes cleaning staff to both the disinfecting agents and, in COVID contexts, to the contaminated environments they're cleaning. UV-C contactless sterilization solves all three problems — but the technology was largely confined to fixed installations or manually pushed trolleys that required an operator present in the irradiated space.

The engineering challenge was to build a mobile robot capable of autonomous navigation through real environments — medical facilities, warehouses, supermarkets, factories, and public spaces — delivering consistent UV-C dosing to surfaces without requiring a human in the room. The system also needed a manual override for situations where human-directed operation was preferable.

Speed was a constraint. The urgency of the COVID-19 context meant the prototype had to be functional and demonstrable in weeks, not the development cycles that typically apply to autonomous mobile systems.

## What I Built

I assembled and led a cross-disciplinary team spanning vision processing, robotics, mechanical engineering, and electronics. The project was structured as a rapid development cycle: parallel workstreams, daily integration checkpoints, and a hard deadline for the functional prototype.

The mechanical architecture was designed for stability and UV-C lamp coverage — the lamp configuration and robot geometry were optimised together to maximise surface irradiance per unit floor area covered. The electronics stack handled motor control, sensor fusion, power management, and the UV-C emitter switching logic.

The vision processing subsystem enabled the robot to navigate real environments: detecting obstacles, mapping traversable space, and executing sterilization passes without predefined routes. The manual override mode allowed an operator to take direct control via remote interface when autonomous navigation was not appropriate for the environment.

The system was designed for deployment across a wide range of facility types — the navigation and dosing parameters were configurable to adapt to different room geometries and surface types.

## The Outcome or Impact

The UV Germicidal Sterilization Robot was completed as a functional prototype and was featured in media coverage of COVID-19 technology responses in Bahrain. The project demonstrated that a small, well-structured engineering team could take a concept from whiteboard to working autonomous system in a compressed timeframe — and that speed doesn't require sacrificing engineering rigour when the team structure is right.

The project also validated Archetype Foundry's capacity to deliver complex mechatronic systems: integrated hardware, firmware, and vision processing, fabricated and tested in-house.
