Most R&D work disappears into a report. This project didn't. The biomechanical concepts and physical prototypes developed at Archetype Foundry fed directly into the Adidas Harden 7 — a globally released performance basketball shoe that hit retail in February 2023. Getting from an idea about forefoot propulsion to a product worn by NBA players is not a straight line, and bridging that gap was the whole challenge.

## The Problem

Elite basketball players generate exceptional forces through the forefoot during explosive lateral cuts, low-center-of-gravity drives, and step-back sequences. Conventional sole constructions treat the forefoot as a passive structure — they resist and absorb load rather than channelling it. The result is wasted propulsive energy and accumulated fatigue across the course of a game.

The brief from Adidas through Archetype Foundry was deliberately open: identify the specific biomechanical limitations in existing basketball footwear around forefoot propulsion, and develop concepts that were concrete enough to evaluate and potentially manufacture. There was no predefined solution. The work had to start with the science.

## What I Built

My background sits at the intersection of biomedical engineering and applied design, and this project drew on both. I began with a kinematic and kinetic analysis of Harden's movement profile — the specific patterns of load transfer, toe-off mechanics, and lateral-to-linear transitions that characterise his playing style. That analysis, grounded in my graduate training in biomedical engineering and clinical biomechanics, let me define precisely where the energy losses were occurring and what structural interventions might address them.

From the analysis I developed a set of design hypotheses: targeted geometry and material configurations in the forefoot zone that could redirect plantar load, improve elastic energy return, and reduce the mechanical mismatch between the shoe's structure and the foot's natural propulsive arc.

I then moved directly into fabrication. Using digital fabrication tools, I produced a series of iterative proof-of-concept prototypes — physical objects that could be handled, worn, measured, and refined. Each cycle tightened the link between the theoretical model and something a foot could actually test under load. By December 2018 we had a prototype set credible enough to present as a structured R&D deliverable to the product development team.

## The Outcome or Impact

The concepts and prototypes developed at Archetype Foundry were integrated into the Adidas Harden 7 product pipeline. The shoe launched commercially in February 2023 — four years after those initial prototypes.

Seeing a biomechanics hypothesis become a manufactured product available globally is an uncommon outcome in consulting R&D. It confirmed the value of the approach: anchor concepts in rigorous movement science, build physical evidence early, and give stakeholders something they can evaluate rather than a slide deck. That methodology — research to prototype to market — has shaped how I approach every product R&D engagement since.
