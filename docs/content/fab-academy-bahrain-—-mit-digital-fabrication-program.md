Fab Academy is not a course about 3D printing. It is a graduate-level, project-based diploma that covers the full digital fabrication stack — from electronics design and embedded programming to CNC machining, composites, and final project integration — delivered simultaneously across a global network of Fab Labs, supervised by instructors qualified by MIT's Fab Foundation. Getting it to Bahrain required qualifying as a regional instructor, building the local delivery infrastructure, and establishing the institutional partnerships needed to make it accessible and funded for Bahraini students.

## The Problem

Bahrain's technology education pipeline had a structural gap at the applied, hands-on level. Universities produced engineers who could simulate and design; what the market increasingly required were engineers who could also fabricate — who understood the translation from digital model to physical object, from circuit schematic to working PCB. That combination of design and fabrication competency was not being systematically developed anywhere in the country.

Fab Academy addresses exactly that gap, but it requires a qualified local instructor and a fully equipped Fab Lab to deliver. It also requires funding — the program is intensive and requires six months of dedicated engagement that most students can't self-fund alongside study or work. Finding the institutional partnership that could make it financially accessible was as important as the pedagogical delivery.

## What I Built

I qualified as a regional instructor under MIT's Fab Foundation — completing the full Fab Academy curriculum myself first, then meeting the standards required to deliver it independently to a local cohort.

I then designed and delivered multiple cycles of the program in Bahrain. The pedagogical model followed the Fab Academy framework: weekly global lectures, local hands-on sessions, weekly student documentation of completed assignments, and a final integrated project that required students to combine skills from across the curriculum into a functioning prototype.

The critical institutional partnership was with Tamkeen — the national Labour and Economy Development Fund. I developed the proposal and negotiated the partnership that registered Fab Academy as a professional certificate under Tamkeen's training and wage support scheme. This made the program financially accessible to university students and recent graduates, who could receive both training funding and wage support while participating — removing the financial barrier that would otherwise have excluded most of the target audience.

Over three cycles, I developed six qualified local instructors who could deliver elements of the curriculum independently — extending the program's capacity beyond what a single instructor can support.

## The Outcome or Impact

The numbers: 175+ participants over three years, 80+ functional prototypes produced, 6 qualified instructors developed. Many participants secured employment directly connected to skills developed in the program; all were able to take an idea to a tangible physical prototype — a threshold that meaningfully changes what is possible for an engineer or designer working in Bahrain.

The Tamkeen partnership was itself a significant outcome. Getting an MIT-originated technical curriculum recognised within a national workforce development funding framework required sustained institutional engagement and a clear demonstration of the program's employment outcomes. The fact that it succeeded means every future cycle of Fab Academy in Bahrain is funded — the access model is structural, not dependent on each cohort finding its own funding.
