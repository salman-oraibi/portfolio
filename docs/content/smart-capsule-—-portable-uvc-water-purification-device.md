Access to safe drinking water on small water vessels — fishing boats, patrol crafts, recreational dinghies — is frequently assumed rather than engineered. Water purification systems designed for fixed installations don't scale down well, and chemical methods carry their own risks in enclosed marine spaces. The Smart Capsule project set out to change that: a point-of-use UVC purification device, compact enough for a small vessel, validated in a biosafety lab, and built to survive real marine conditions.

## The Problem

The Arabian Gulf Bio-Remedies client needed a purification solution that could be deployed on small water vessels without requiring shore power, complex installation, or specialist maintenance. Existing point-of-use UV systems were either too bulky, too power-hungry, or designed for fixed freshwater plumbing — none were suited to the compact, portable, marine-environment use case.

The engineering brief covered everything from concept feasibility through to high-fidelity prototype and lab testing. The device had to fit a compact form factor, maintain reliable UVC dosing across variable flow conditions, and operate from onboard vessel power sources. Lab validation against common water contamination microbes was a hard requirement before the client could proceed to user testing.

## What I Built

The project ran across two sites — Archetype Foundry for detailed design engineering and Fab Lab Bahrain for electronic circuit design, fabrication, and prototype iteration.

I led the product development process end-to-end. Starting from concept feasibility, I developed the device architecture: the optical geometry of the UVC chamber, the flow path design to ensure adequate exposure time, the housing geometry for portability and marine durability, and the electronics specification for the UVC driver, power management, and dosing control circuit.

The electronic circuit design and fabrication work was done in-house at Fab Lab Bahrain. I oversaw the development of the PCB layout and the fabrication of four functional prototypes — each iterated based on test data from the previous version. The prototypes were designed to operate within the power envelope of small vessel systems and to withstand salt air and moisture exposure.

Lab validation was conducted at a bio-lab, running standardised contamination challenges against the device's treatment output.

## The Outcome or Impact

The validation results were definitive: the Smart Capsule achieved a 99.9% reduction of common water contamination microbes within a 15-minute treatment cycle. Four functional prototypes were produced, giving the client sufficient units to conduct user testing with real end users in real marine environments.

The project is a clear example of what happens when research-grade engineering meets accessible fabrication infrastructure. The Fab Lab's digital fabrication and electronics capability allowed rapid, low-cost prototype iteration that would have been prohibitively expensive through conventional contract manufacturing routes — and the lab validation gave the client the confidence to take the device directly to user testing.
