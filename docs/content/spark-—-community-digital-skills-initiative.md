The most efficient way to scale educational impact is to train the trainers — but that only works if the trainers have the right tools, the right content, and the infrastructure to deliver independently. SPARK was designed around that premise: a community empowerment initiative that leveraged Fab Lab Bahrain's alumni network as local ambassadors, equipped them to run digital skills workshops in their own neighbourhoods, and connected everything through an online platform purpose-built for the program.

## The Problem

Digital skills education in Bahrain is heavily centralised. Programs run from institutions — universities, the Fab Lab, government training centres — and participants have to come to the facility. That model works for motivated adults who can commit time and travel. It doesn't reach children aged 8–14 in diverse regions across the island, particularly in communities without easy access to central hubs.

The gap wasn't content — Fab Lab Bahrain had an extensive curriculum. The gap was delivery reach. The programme needed a distributed model: trained local educators who could take workshop activities into their communities, supported by portable resources and a digital platform that removed the dependency on centralised infrastructure.

## What I Built

SPARK's architecture had three components: the ambassador training program, the educational content and toolkits, and the online platform.

I led the design of the ambassador training program, selecting and developing Fab Lab alumni as community trainers — people who already had the technical foundation and now needed the pedagogical tools and resources to teach others. The training covered educational facilitation, the use of the electronics kits and programming materials, and the online platform that would support their delivery.

I oversaw the design of 35+ curriculum lessons, aligned to the Fab Lab Bahrain content but adapted for the 8–14 age group and the community workshop format — shorter sessions, lower prerequisite knowledge, and higher emphasis on playful, project-based activities aligned to the Sustainable Development Goals. Every lesson was built to be deliverable with the program's portable materials kit, without specialist equipment.

The online platform provided trainers with structured guidance, downloadable materials, and an integrated development environment for the programming activities — making all resources accessible from anywhere and ensuring consistency of delivery across the ambassador network.

## The Outcome or Impact

In the 2023–24 academic year, 20 trained trainers and volunteers delivered SPARK workshops to over 400 students across diverse regions of Bahrain. The US Embassy partnership provided both funding and institutional credibility that supported the programme's public presence and community reach.

SPARK demonstrated that a community-based delivery model — trained ambassadors, portable materials, digital platform — can achieve coverage that a centralised facility model cannot. The 400 students reached in year one represent a fraction of what the model can scale to as the ambassador network grows. The constraint is not infrastructure; it is trainer capacity, and that is a problem the program is designed to solve from within its own alumni community.
