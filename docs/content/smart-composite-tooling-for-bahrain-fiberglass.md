Composites manufacturing is a field where the tooling that shapes a product can cost more than the product itself. Complex topologies — the curves, undercuts, and compound geometries that make composite parts structurally efficient — demand moulds that are time-intensive to design and expensive to fabricate at scale. When Bahrain Fiberglass came to Archetype Foundry looking for a better way, I led the research and development effort to build one.

## The Problem

Traditional tooling for large-scale composite articles relies on machined metal or hand-laid fibreglass moulds. These approaches work, but they're expensive, slow to iterate, and brittle when the design changes — which it always does. The core challenge was to find a way to rapidly create tooling for complex topologies without sacrificing the dimensional accuracy or surface quality that composite layup demands.

The brief covered the full scope: concept research, prototyping, simulation, and delivery of a minimum viable product with both hardware and software components. There was no off-the-shelf answer. This was a genuine R&D problem.

## What I Built

I approached the project through three parallel tracks: visualization and simulation, design and engineering, and physical fabrication.

On the research side, I mapped the failure modes of existing tooling approaches and identified the specific geometric and material constraints that drove cost. That analysis shaped the concept: a modular, reconfigurable tooling system capable of producing complex topology forms without the lead time or expense of machined hard tooling.

I developed and iterated CAD models and ran structural and thermal simulations to validate the concept before committing to physical prototypes. The simulation phase was critical — it let us eliminate weak design directions early and focus fabrication effort on configurations with real promise.

On the hardware side, I designed and fabricated the MVP prototype using digital fabrication equipment, integrating both the mechanical tooling structure and the embedded components needed for monitoring and control. The software layer — written to interface with the hardware — enabled real-time feedback during the composite layup process.

Throughout, I ran the project using Agile methodology: short cycles, early physical evidence, and regular stakeholder checkpoints to keep the development aligned with Bahrain Fiberglass's manufacturing requirements.

## The Outcome or Impact

The project delivered a working MVP: a tooling solution capable of producing complex composite topologies at materially lower cost and with faster iteration cycles than conventional approaches. The prototype demonstrated the core concept under realistic conditions and gave Bahrain Fiberglass a concrete platform for further development and scale-up.

For Archetype Foundry, it reinforced a model of R&D consulting that goes beyond recommendations — we build the thing, we test it, and we hand over something a manufacturing partner can actually use.
