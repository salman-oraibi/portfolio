Academic research in chemical process intensification frequently stalls at the prototype stage. Designing a microreactor geometry is a computational and chemical problem; fabricating one with the precision required for meaningful experimental results is a manufacturing problem. Most university labs aren't equipped for both. Fab Lab Bahrain's digital fabrication infrastructure filled that gap for the University of Bahrain's Chemical Engineering Department — and what started as a technical collaboration became a multi-year research partnership with published results and 15 trained student engineers.

## The Problem

Conventional biodiesel production is energy-intensive and cost-heavy at small to medium scale. Microreactors — miniaturised flow chemistry platforms — offer a path to process intensification: higher yields, lower energy consumption, and reduced reagent waste relative to batch processing. But the research required to validate microreactor geometries for biodiesel production demands physical prototypes with precise internal geometries, made from materials chemically compatible with the reaction environment.

The University of Bahrain's Chemical Engineering Department had the research expertise but not the fabrication capability. Designing multiple microreactor variants — different geometries, different channel configurations — and fabricating them with sufficient precision for comparative experimental testing required access to high-resolution laser cutting and fine milling equipment, and the process knowledge to use it.

## What I Built

I led the fabrication collaboration, working with Chemical Engineering faculty and student researchers to translate their microreactor designs into physical devices. The fabrication program used a high-resolution laser cutting machine and fine milling to produce microreactors with different geometries — internal channel configurations designed to test the effect of flow path geometry on reaction efficiency and yield.

Multiple reactor variants were designed and fabricated across the research program. Each fabrication run involved close collaboration with the student researchers: reviewing design drawings, advising on fabrication constraints that affected geometric feasibility, and adjusting designs iteratively to improve fabrication quality.

In parallel with the fabrication work, I supervised 15 student engineers through the process — from reading and adapting CAD drawings for fabrication, to operating the laser cutting and milling equipment, to troubleshooting and validating completed devices before experimental testing. By the end of the program, all 15 students could design and fabricate microreactors independently.

## The Outcome or Impact

The collaboration produced results at three levels. Scientifically: a peer-reviewed publication in the Journal of Chemical Engineering and Processing — Process Intensification, validating the research contribution. Programmatically: five senior engineering projects completed with physical microreactor prototypes, a level of tangible output rarely achieved in academic final year projects. Institutionally: 15 trained engineers with direct hands-on microreactor design and fabrication capability — a cohort who leave university able to work at the intersection of process chemistry and precision fabrication.

The project reinforced a core principle of Fab Lab Bahrain's academic collaboration model: the most valuable thing we can offer a university research program is not access to machines, but the process knowledge and fabrication expertise to turn research designs into testable physical artefacts — and the structured supervision to develop student capability alongside the research output.
