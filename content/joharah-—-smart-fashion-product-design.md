The intersection of fashion and technology rarely produces products that take both seriously. Most "smart" fashion accessories make the technology visible and the aesthetics an afterthought, or they bury the hardware so deeply that the functionality becomes unreliable. Joharah was conceived to do neither. I led the detailed design engineering for a connected fashion product that carries real personal protection and tracking features — and looks like it belongs in a jewellery collection, not a tech showcase.

## The Problem

The client came with a specific user in mind: someone who wanted the security of personal tracking and emergency functions without wearing something that signals vulnerability or broadcasts technology. The product had to perform — location tracking, personal security triggers, mobile connectivity — while looking and feeling like a piece of considered fashion design.

That dual requirement drove every engineering decision. The form factor had to be compact and jewellery-like. The electronics had to fit without compromising the aesthetic. The mobile application had to be intuitive enough to be used under stress. And the entire product had to be designed to manufacturing tolerances so it could actually be produced at scale.

## What I Built

I led the detailed engineering phase across the mechanical, electronic, and software dimensions of the product.

On the hardware side, I developed the enclosure and mechanical design to meet both the aesthetic and structural requirements — a geometry that accommodates the electronics and battery within a form factor appropriate for fashion wear. I specified the embedded system architecture: the microcontroller, communication module, sensor suite, and power management components, then validated the layout through iterative prototyping.

The connected mobile application was designed to pair seamlessly with the device, providing real-time location visibility, emergency alert triggers, and status monitoring. The UX was designed around high-stress use cases — the interaction model had to work on the first attempt, without instruction, in a moment of urgency.

The full product development cycle concluded with manufacturing-ready documentation: engineering drawings, component specifications, assembly guides, and material callouts sufficient for a contract manufacturer to begin production.

## The Outcome or Impact

Joharah reached manufacturing-ready status — a complete, validated design with full documentation for production scale-up. The project demonstrated that connected hardware products can meet high aesthetic standards without compromising functionality, and that the full product development pipeline — concept through manufacturing handoff — could be delivered within a consulting R&D framework.

The project also reinforced the importance of user research and cultural context in connected product design. The security and tracking features were shaped directly by an understanding of the target user's context, not just by what the hardware could technically support.
