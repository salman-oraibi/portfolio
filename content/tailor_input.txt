### Biomechanics Research for the Adidas Harden 7
Most R&D work disappears into a report. This project didn't. The biomechanical concepts and physical prototypes developed at Archetype Foundry fed directly into the Adidas Harden 7 — a globally released performance basketball shoe that hit retail in February 2023. Getting from an idea about forefoot propulsion to a product worn by NBA players is not a straight line, and bridging that gap was the whole challenge.

## The Problem

Elite basketball players generate exceptional forces through the forefoot during explosive lateral cuts, low-center-of-gravity drives, and step-back sequences. Conventional sole constructions treat the forefoot as a passive structure — they resist and absorb load rather than channelling it. The result is wasted propulsive energy and accumulated fatigue across the course of a game.

The brief from Adidas through Archetype Foundry was deliberately open: identify the specific biomechanical limitations in existing basketball footwear around forefoot propulsion, and develop concepts that were concrete enough to evaluate and potentially manufacture. There was no predefined solution. The work had to start with the science.

## What I Built

My background sits at the intersection of biomedical engineering and applied design, and this project drew on both. I began with a kinematic and kinetic analysis of Harden's movement profile — the specific patterns of load transfer, toe-off mechanics, and lateral-to-linear transitions that characterise his playing style.

## The Problem

Bahrain's technology education pipeline had a structural gap at the applied, hands-on level. Universities produced engineers who could simulate and design; what the market increasingly required were engineers who could also fabricate — who understood the translation from digital model to physical object, from circuit schematic to working PCB. That combination of design and fabrication competency was not being systematically developed anywhere in the country.

Fab Academy addresses exactly that gap, but it requires a qualified local instructor and a fully equipped Fab Lab to deliver. ### TechStride — Empowering Bahraini Youth with Digital Skills
Most technology awareness programs for young people are passive. Students attend, they watch, they listen, and they leave with an appreciation of technologies they didn't touch. TechStride was built on a different premise: that the students most likely to pursue careers in industrial robotics, AI, or game design are the ones who have actually built something — who have experienced the moment when an idea becomes a prototype, and who can connect that experience to a career direction. I led the development and delivery of TechStride at Fab Lab Bahrain as a project-centred intensive program for high school students and fresh university entrants.

## The Problem

Bahrain's transition to an innovation economy requires a pipeline of technically capable young people entering university with a genuine interest in technology disciplines — not just students who selected engineering because it seemed employable. The challenge is exposure and experience: most Bahraini students reach their university application having never operated industrial equipment, written a line of code for a physical system, or designed something that moves.

TechStride was designed to address that gap through intensive, project-driven experience across industrial robotics, AI, machine learning, and game design. The target audience was high school students and fresh university entrants — young people at the exact decision point where a compelling hands-on experience can redirect academic and career choices.
