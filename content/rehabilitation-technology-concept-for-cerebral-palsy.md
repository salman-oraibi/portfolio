Designing rehabilitation technology for cerebral palsy patients is not a hardware problem first. It's a biomechanics problem, an anthropometry problem, and a human factors problem — and only once those are resolved does it become a hardware problem. When a Bahraini startup developing CP rehabilitation therapy technology engaged Archetype Foundry for concept design and research, I led the work that built the scientific foundation their product needed before any engineering began.

## The Problem

Cerebral palsy presents highly variable motor profiles across patients — spasticity patterns, range of motion limitations, asymmetries, and postural compensations differ significantly between individuals. A rehabilitation device that doesn't account for this variability risks being ineffective at best, or causing secondary injury at worst.

The startup had an innovation idea and clinical intent but needed rigorous biomechanical and ergonomic grounding before committing to a design direction. The engagement called for concept design research across biomechanics, anthropometry, and ergonomics — delivered through use case scenarios and simulations, not just literature review. Full concept feasibility and human factors analysis were required before any physical prototype work could begin.

## What I Built

Drawing on my clinical training and graduate work in biomedical engineering, I led the research program from first principles. I mapped the motor impairment profiles most relevant to the target patient population, established the anthropometric envelope the device needed to accommodate, and identified the postural and kinematic constraints that would govern the mechanical design.

The human factors analysis went beyond standard ergonomics. I developed specific use case scenarios — the clinical interaction between therapist, device, and patient — and ran through them analytically and through simulation to identify friction points, safety considerations, and the design parameters most sensitive to patient variability. The goal was to understand not just whether the device could be used, but how it would actually behave across the range of patients it would encounter.

The full concept feasibility report integrated the biomechanical, anthropometric, and human factors findings into a set of design specifications and constraints: the envelope the device had to fit, the forces it could and couldn't apply, the adjustability required to span the patient population, and the interaction model that would make it usable in a clinical setting.

## The Outcome or Impact

The concept feasibility work gave the startup a biomechanically grounded foundation for their first physical prototype. Rather than building to intuition and discovering clinical limitations during testing, they went into hardware development with a clear specification derived from real patient biomechanics and human factors analysis.

This is the work that doesn't appear in product portfolios — it happens before the thing that ships. But it's the work that determines whether the thing that ships is safe, effective, and clinically credible. For a CP rehabilitation device, getting that foundation right wasn't optional.
