The bottleneck in technology education isn't access to machines — it's educators who know how to use them pedagogically. Putting a 3D printer in a school classroom changes nothing if the teacher has no framework for turning it into learning. Fab'n Box was designed to solve that problem: a Train-The-Trainers program for educators in Bahrain's technical and vocational public schools, delivered in partnership with the Ministry of Education and the American Embassy, with a portable digital fabrication kit designed to enable ad-hoc maker activities anywhere.

## The Problem

Bahrain's technical and vocational public schools had STEAM mandates but lacked educators trained to deliver digital fabrication activities with confidence. The Fab Lab's infrastructure was purpose-built for this kind of learning, but it wasn't portable — a class couldn't just come to the Fab Lab when they needed it, and the Fab Lab couldn't relocate to every school.

The challenge was to design a program that would train teachers as practitioners first, then give them a portable toolkit that let them run digital fabrication activities in their own classrooms without needing access to a full Fab Lab. The content had to be pedagogically sound, practically achievable with the toolbox, and adaptable to different science and technology topics.

## What We Delivered

I led the design and development of the full program curriculum and content, structuring it around digital fabrication principles and applied project-based learning activities. I directed the curriculum team to ensure the material was immediately actionable by educators with limited prior fabrication experience, progressing from fundamentals to independent lesson delivery.

I oversaw the design of the Fab'n Box toolbox in parallel with the curriculum. I coordinated the team responsible for specifying and assembling the kit — compact equipment, consumables, activity guides, and instructor materials. I directed the pedagogical design work to ensure that the activities in the box mapped directly to the training curriculum, so educators could move from the program into delivery without a translation gap.

Under my leadership, three MiniLABs were established, staffed by seven trained trainers, and the program reached over 450 students in its initial deployment across technical and vocational schools.

## The Outcome or Impact

Fab'n Box established a scalable model for digital fabrication education in public schools that doesn't depend on school-level infrastructure investment. The trained educators can now run meaningful STEAM activities independently, in their own classrooms, with the toolbox as their enabling platform. The program's portability means coverage is limited by trainer capacity, not by equipment availability.

The partnership I coordinated with the Ministry of Education and the American Embassy gave the program institutional legitimacy and reach that a standalone Fab Lab initiative couldn't achieve independently — and demonstrated that a well-designed curriculum and portable toolkit can extend the Fab Lab's educational impact far beyond its physical location.
