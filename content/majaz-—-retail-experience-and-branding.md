The best retail environments don't feel like retail. They feel like places people want to be — spaces where the boundary between shopping, dining, socialising, and simply existing outdoors dissolves into a coherent experience. That was the design ambition for MAJAZ: a hybrid indoor/outdoor mall concept commissioned by Adel Abdullah Al-Shoaibi Real Estate in Saudi Arabia. I led the architectural concept design, the branding identity, and the visualization output that made the project communicable to stakeholders.

## The Problem

The client needed more than architectural drawings. They needed a compelling vision — one that could be communicated to investors, tenants, and planning authorities at a level of detail sufficient to generate buy-in, while remaining flexible enough to evolve through the development process.

The hybrid indoor/outdoor format presented specific design challenges. The climate envelope for a Saudi Arabian retail environment has to be managed carefully — outdoor spaces need shading, airflow, and microclimate design to be usable for more than a few weeks per year. The architectural concept had to solve the environmental problem while preserving the experiential open quality the client wanted.

At the same time, the branding work had to give MAJAZ an identity distinct from existing Gulf mall developments — something that communicated the hybrid character of the space in a name, a visual language, and a story.

## What I Built

I led the architectural concept design process from initial site analysis through to a fully visualised concept package. The spatial planning established the hybrid layout — anchored indoor retail zones connected through climatically designed outdoor corridors and open-air gathering spaces, with shade structures and landscape elements that extended the usable environment year-round.

The visualization work translated the architectural concept into rendered environments communicable to non-technical stakeholders: exterior perspectives, interior mood renders, and spatial sequences that conveyed the experience of moving through the development. These weren't technical drawings — they were persuasion tools, designed to make the vision tangible before a foundation was poured.

In parallel, I developed the MAJAZ brand: name development, visual identity, typographic system, and brand guidelines. The identity was designed to reflect the hybrid nature of the space — a word that carries connotations of passage, transition, and connection in Arabic, mapped onto a visual language that referenced both traditional regional design and contemporary retail aesthetics.

## The Outcome or Impact

The MAJAZ concept package gave the client everything needed to proceed with investor presentations and planning submissions. The architectural concept, branding, and visualization output formed a coherent development narrative that communicated the project's ambition clearly and credibly.

The project demonstrated that design strategy and architectural concept work are not separate disciplines. The brand identity was developed in parallel with the spatial concept — each informing the other — and the result was a project identity that was architecturally grounded, not a naming exercise bolted on at the end.
