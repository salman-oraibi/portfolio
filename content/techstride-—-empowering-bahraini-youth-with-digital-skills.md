Most technology awareness programs for young people are passive. Students attend, they watch, they listen, and they leave with an appreciation of technologies they didn't touch. TechStride was built on a different premise: that the students most likely to pursue careers in industrial robotics, AI, or game design are the ones who have actually built something — who have experienced the moment when an idea becomes a prototype, and who can connect that experience to a career direction. I led the development and delivery of TechStride at Fab Lab Bahrain as a project-centred intensive program for high school students and fresh university entrants.

## The Problem

Bahrain's transition to an innovation economy requires a pipeline of technically capable young people entering university with a genuine interest in technology disciplines — not just students who selected engineering because it seemed employable. The challenge is exposure and experience: most Bahraini students reach their university application having never operated industrial equipment, written a line of code for a physical system, or designed something that moves.

TechStride was designed to address that gap through intensive, project-driven experience across industrial robotics, AI, machine learning, and game design. The target audience was high school students and fresh university entrants — young people at the exact decision point where a compelling hands-on experience can redirect academic and career choices.

## What I Built

I led the program design and delivery, working with a team of seven qualified instructors to develop and run TechStride as a project-centred intensive.

The curriculum structure combined theoretical orientation with hands-on project work across four technology domains: industrial robotics operation and programming, AI and machine learning fundamentals applied to real datasets, game design and development, and integrated project work that drew on multiple domains. Each domain was structured as a learning arc from first contact to functional output — participants didn't just learn about industrial robots, they programmed one.

The project component was central to the program philosophy. Every participant was expected to produce a tangible prototype by the end of the intensive — a physical or digital artefact that demonstrated applied understanding of the technology domain they had engaged with most deeply. The instructors were structured as mentors as much as teachers: guiding project development, troubleshooting, and helping participants connect their specific project to broader career pathways.

The program also incorporated explicit career orientation — structured conversations about how the skills developed in TechStride map to university majors, industry roles, and innovation pathways, so participants left with more than a prototype.

## The Outcome or Impact

In 2024, TechStride's single-month delivery produced 60+ participants, 20+ project prototypes, and — by participant self-report — meaningful shifts in approach to idea generation, project development, and career direction. Seven qualified instructors were developed through the program's delivery cycle.

The most significant outcome is the hardest to measure: the participants who chose a technical major because of what they built in TechStride, or who arrived at university already knowing how to think through a project from problem to prototype. That pipeline effect — changing the inputs to Bahrain's higher education system — is what TechStride was designed to create.
